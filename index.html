Overview of the Final Project

Your final project will be to create a webpage on the class Canvas site (“Pages” in the left side menu) through which you will explore the relationship between Wollstonecraft, Walker or Burke and one other text from this quarter (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, or, if using Wollstonecraft or Walker, Burke).

You will do this by using one of the artworks in the Wise to Power exhibition at the Smart Museum as a sort of bridge between the texts. How does the artwork illustrate the critique or engagement that either Wollstonecraft, Walker or Burke offer with regard to the ideas found in one of the other works? How does the piece reflect what Wollstonecraft, Walker or Burke might be said to critique, modify, or comment on in one of the other political theories we’ve read?

This is not about just finding agreement or disagreement between the art and/or the theories – it’s about putting the three into conversation with each other. Does Wollstonecraft, Walker or Burke offer some commentary on or engagement with another theorist that can be interpreted, reflected or expressed through the art piece?

You are welcome to use whatever media are available to you via the webpage (text, image, video, music, etc.).

Interpreting Art

You will need to include an analysis of both the visual evidence and the textual evidence, and strong projects will use visual evidence to analyze and interpret the artwork. This will require looking closely at the piece.

Advice for describing and analyzing the visual evidence

  • Do not rely solely on the object label in the museum for your interpretation – think about an interpretation that is (a) rooted in the visual evidence presented and (b) related to a central theme the authors address.
  • Each of the visual elements in a work is important. Consider the figures, the setting, the artist’s use of media, composition, brushwork, narrative, and elements such as color, light, line, scale, depth, texture, space, facial expressions, etc. Visual analysis employs description of what you see to make interpretive assertions about a work of visual art.
  • Think about how the artist has depicted the subject of the theme(s) you notice in the work and wish to analyze in your project.
  • Try to account for as many meaningful visual details as you can.

NB: You will need to return to the museum to examine your piece in more detail; do not rely solely on your observations during class time, and do not rely on photographs.
